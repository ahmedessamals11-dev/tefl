Module 01

Foundations of Communicative Language Teaching

Communicative Competence (Canale & Swain Model)

Definition: Communicative Competence is the internalized, tacit knowledge of language rules and the ability to use that knowledge for meaningful communication. It is what you know about language and its appropriate use.

A. Grammatical Competence — Mastery of the linguistic code including vocabulary, word formation, sentence structure, pronunciation, spelling, and semantics. While traditional methods treated grammar as the goal, CLT treats grammatical competence as a resource for communication. It is necessary but not sufficient.

B. Sociolinguistic Competence — The ability to understand and produce language that is socially appropriate in a given context.

DimensionExplanationExample
RegisterFormal vs. informal language"I require assistance" vs. "I need help"
PolitenessFace-saving strategies"Could you possibly…?" vs. "Open the door"
Cultural normsWhat is taboo or preferredEye contact, personal space, greetings
Speech actsApologizing, requesting, refusingSaying "I'm sorry" differently across cultures

C. Discourse Competence — The ability to connect sentences or utterances into a coherent, cohesive whole—whether spoken or written. Includes cohesion (surface-level connections like reference pronouns, conjunctions) and coherence (logical meaning, topic maintenance, turn-taking).

D. Strategic Competence — The ability to use verbal and non-verbal communication strategies to compensate for breakdowns or enhance the message.

  • Achievement Strategies: Paraphrase, word coinage, circumlocution, code-switching, non-verbal communication
  • Reduction Strategies: Message abandonment, topic avoidance, meaning replacement

Competence vs. Performance (Chomsky, 1965)

CompetencePerformance
Tacit knowledge of the language systemActual use of language in real situations
What you know (internal grammar)What you do (speech/writing)
Ideal, error-freeFull of slips, false starts, hesitations
StableVariable (fatigue, anxiety, distraction affect it)

Hymes' Critique (1972): "There are rules of use without which the rules of grammar are useless." A child could generate infinite grammatically correct sentences but still be unable to communicate appropriately. Thus, Communicative Competence = Grammatical Competence + Sociolinguistic Competence.

Fluency vs. Accuracy

AccuracyFluency
Focus on formFocus on meaning
Error prevention/correctionError tolerance
Controlled practiceFree production
Analytical, rule-basedAutomatic, holistic

Interlanguage Perspective (Selinker, 1972): Learners develop an interlanguage—a unique, rule-governed linguistic system that evolves over time.

CLT Balance: Task stage = fluency focus (delayed correction); Language Focus stage = accuracy focus (immediate correction).

Teacher and Learner Roles in CLT

Teacher Roles (Breen & Candlin, 1980):

  • Facilitator: Creates opportunities for communication, provides resources and scaffolds
  • Needs Analyst: Conducts needs analysis, adapts syllabus based on learner goals
  • Counselor/Consultant: Advises individuals during tasks, responds to student-initiated queries
  • Co-communicator: Participates in discussions as an equal, shares opinions
  • Classroom Manager: Organizes physical space, manages time and transitions

Learner Roles:

  • Negotiator of Meaning: Clarifies, confirms, paraphrases
  • Collaborator: Co-constructs meaning with peers, provides peer feedback
  • Risk-Taker: Attempts unfamiliar structures, guesses from context
  • Self-Evaluator: Reflects on performance, identifies strengths/weaknesses

Characteristics of Communicative Tasks

CriterionDescription
Primary focus on meaningLearners attend to message content

Module 02

Lesson Planning & SMART Objectives

SMART Framework

S - Specific: "Students will write 5 sentences describing what they did yesterday using regular -ed past forms." (Not: "Students will understand the past tense.")

M - Measurable: Evidence must be observable. Use Bloom's Taxonomy action verbs: identify, classify, role-play, justify, create.

A - Achievable: Realistic given time, level, resources. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): challenging but possible with support.

R - Relevant: Aligned with learner needs and course goals. For CLT: relevance = real-world applicability.

T - Time-bound: Achievable within one lesson or defined period. "By the end of the 50-minute session..."

Observable Learning Outcomes

Non-observable (Cognitive): Know, understand, appreciate, learn, realize

Observable (Performance): List, describe, demonstrate, create, compare, negotiate

CLT Application: ❌ "Students will understand how to apologize." ✅ "Students will role-play a scenario apologizing for being late, using at least two apology strategies (excuse, offer to fix)."

Lesson Planning Framework for CLT

Traditional PPP: Present → Practice → Produce

CLT Task-Based Learning (Willis, 1996):

  • Pre-task: Introduce topic, activate language, model task
  • Task Cycle:
    • Task: Students do task in pairs/groups (fluency)
    • Planning: Prepare oral/written report
    • Report: Present findings to class
  • Language Focus:
    • Analysis: Examine language features from task
    • Practice: Pronunciation, form drilling (accuracy)
Module 03

Designing Communicative Activities

Task-Based Learning (TBL) Principles

Principle 1: Holistic Language Use — Language is acquired whole-to-part, not part-to-whole. Learners encounter complete texts/discourse, not isolated sentences.

Principle 2: Intrinsic Motivation — Task success is inherently satisfying. Example: Successfully booking a hotel room in English is motivating in itself.

Principle 3: Learnability vs. Acquisition — Teaching a rule does not guarantee acquisition. Acquisition requires processing meaning first.

Principle 4: Form-Meaning Mapping — Learners need to see how grammatical choices affect meaning. Why choose passive voice? ("The window was broken" vs. "John broke the window")

Information Gap Activities

TypeDescriptionExample
One-way gapA has info; B doesn'tA describes picture; B draws
Two-way gapA has some info; B has complementary infoSpot-the-difference
Opinion gapNo right/wrong; personal preference"Should schools require uniforms?"

Problem-Solving Tasks

Task TypeCognitive DemandExample
RankingLowRank weekend activities
SequencingMediumPut story pictures in order
PrioritizingMedium-HighSurvival on a desert island
Case studyHighSolve a business problem
DilemmaHighEthical choices

Communicative Task Checklist

Rate 0-3:

  1. Do students speak more than the teacher?
  2. Is there a genuine transfer of information?
  3. Is the outcome a completed task, not just language practice?
  4. Do students work in pairs/groups?
  5. Is the language use unscripted?
  6. Does the task resemble real life?
Score <12 = Not fully communicative. Adapt it.
Module 04

Teaching Language Communicatively

Meaning → Form → Pronunciation

Stage 1: Meaning (Contextualize) — Present target language in a text, dialogue, or video. Check comprehension of message, not form. Example: Students read a restaurant review. Teacher asks: "Did they like the food? Why?"

Stage 2: Form (Notice) — Students search text for specific language. Example: "Underline how the writer expresses opinions. Circle adjectives used." Learners hypothesize rules; teacher confirms/clarifies.

Stage 3: Pronunciation (Personalize) — Drill in meaningful phrases, not isolation. Connect to learners' own experiences. Example: Students say their own restaurant opinions with correct intonation.

Inductive vs. Deductive Teaching

AspectDeductiveInductive
DirectionRule → ExamplesExamples → Rule
Cognitive loadLow (receive)High (discover)
RetentionLowerHigher
Time efficiencyFasterSlower
Learner autonomyLowerHigher

CLT Stance: Inductive is preferred for adults and intermediate+ learners. Deductive may be used for complex, exception-heavy rules.

Pre/While/Post Framework for Skills

PRE (Activate): Predict from title/images, brainstorm vocabulary, set purpose: "Read to find out..."

WHILE (Navigate):

  • Gist: First listen: general idea
  • Scan: Find specific details
  • Intensive: Infer meaning, analyze language

POST (Transfer): Discussion, debate, creative writing, role-play

Lexical Chunks and Collocations

The Lexical Approach (Michael Lewis, 1993): "Language consists of grammaticalized lexis, not lexicalized grammar."

TypeDefinitionExample
PolywordsFixed phrases"by the way", "in the end"
CollocationsWords that co-occur"make a decision", "heavy rain"
Institutionalized utterancesEntire sentences"There's a first time for everything"
Sentence framesDiscourse organizers"Firstly... Secondly... Finally..."

CLT Implication: Teach chunks, not just single words. Fluency comes from accessing prefabricated language, not generating every sentence from scratch.

Module 05

Classroom Interaction & Teacher Role

Teacher Talking Time (TTT) vs. Student Talking Time (STT)

Typical TTT in traditional class: 70-80%

Target TTT in CLT: 20-30%

Where TTT Hides: Giving lengthy instructions, echoing student answers ("Good. John said..."), asking display questions (known answers), lecturing grammar rules

STT Maximizers: Pair/group work, open-ended questions, communication games, student presentations

Pair and Group Work Management

Instruction-Checking Questions (ICQs): Instead of "Do you understand?" ask:

  • "In this task, do you write or speak?"
  • "How many minutes do you have?"
  • "Who goes first?"

Group Roles:

  • Facilitator: Keeps group on task
  • Spokesperson: Reports to class
  • Timekeeper: Monitors time
  • Secretary: Writes notes

Scaffolding Techniques

Module 06

Materials & Task Design

Authentic Materials

Categories:

  • Print: Menus, brochures, receipts, schedules
  • Audio: Podcasts, radio, voicemail, songs
  • Audio-visual: News clips, YouTube, films
  • Digital: Websites, social media, emails

Why Authentic?

  1. Exposure to real discourse features (hesitations, false starts)
  2. Cultural content embedded
  3. Motivational (adult learners value real texts)
  4. Preparation for independent real-world navigation

Task Outcomes and Real-World Relevance

Non-communicative outcome: "Complete the worksheet." / "Answer the questions."

Communicative outcome: "Choose a restaurant for the team dinner based on the reviews." / "Decide which candidate to hire."

Real-World Transfer: The task in class should mirror a task outside class.

Materials Evaluation and Adaptation

ProblemCLT Adaptation
Textbook has drills onlyAdd an information gap stage
No pair workInsert "Discuss with partner" prompts
Fake textsReplace with authentic text, keep same topic
Passive receptionAdd pre-reading prediction task
No personalizationAdd "What about you?" section
Module 07

Assessment in Communicative Language Teaching

Formative vs. Summative Assessment

DimensionFormativeSummative
TimingDuring learningEnd of unit/course
PurposeImprove, adjust, supportMeasure, grade, certify
StakesLowHigh
FeedbackDescriptive, immediateJudgmental, delayed
ExampleObservation, peer feedback, draftsFinal exam, final presentation

CLT Emphasis: Strong formative assessment culture. Students need feedback on communicative attempts, not just final grades.

Performance-Based Assessment

Definition: Assessment requiring demonstration of knowledge through real-world tasks.

Characteristics:

  • Open-ended
  • Process and product evaluated
  • Criteria shared in advance
  • Self/peer involvement

CLT Examples: Conduct a job interview, leave a voicemail message, write a complaint email, present a proposal

Can-Do Statements

Structure: "I can [action] in [context] with [quality]."

LevelExample
A1"I can order a drink at a café."
B1"I can describe a travel experience in detail."
C1"I can argue a complex position in a debate."

Assessment Use: Learners self-assess against can-do lists; teacher observes and confirms.

Peer and Self-Assessment Strategies

Peer Assessment Structures:

  • Two stars and a wish: Two positives, one suggestion
  • Gallery walk: Post work, peers leave sticky note feedback
  • Checklist: Criteria-based observation during presentations

Self-Assessment Tools:

  • Exit tickets: "Today I learned... I still need..."
  • Learning logs: Weekly reflection entries
  • Portfolio reflections: Curated work with commentary
Module 08

Reflection & Professional Growth

Reflective Journals

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988):

  1. Description: What happened?
  2. Feelings: What were you thinking/feeling?
  3. Evaluation: What was good/bad?
  4. Analysis: What sense can you make of it?
  5. Conclusion: What else could you have done?
  6. Action Plan: If it arises again, what will you do?

CLT Focus Questions:

  • Did students communicate genuinely or just "do the activity"?
  • Who talked more—me or them?
  • Did I correct errors at the right time?

Peer Observation Checklists

Sample CLT Observation Criteria:

  • Teacher talk ≤ 30% of time?
  • Instructions: Clear? ICQs used?
  • Task: Is there a gap? Outcome?
  • Error correction: Appropriate timing?
  • Student interaction: Pair/group effective?

Non-judgmental language:

  • "I noticed..." (not "You failed to...")
  • "What was your intention when...?"
  • "Have you considered...?"

Action Planning

SMART for Teachers:

  • S: "I will reduce TTT by using more pair work."
  • M: "I will time myself in 3 lessons; target <15 min TTT."
  • A: "I will start with one pair activity per lesson."
  • R: "My students need more speaking time."
  • T: "By the end of this month."

Introduction to Action Research

Cycle:

  1. Identify problem: "My intermediate students avoid speaking."
  2. Plan intervention: "I will implement role-plays for 4 weeks."
  3. Act/Observe: Video record lessons; student surveys.
  4. Reflect: Analyze data; what changed?
  5. Revise: Adjust approach; cycle continues.

CLT Action Research Examples:

  • Does pre-task planning time improve fluency?
  • Do peer checklists improve student self-correction?
  • Are information gap tasks more engaging than textbook dialogues?
